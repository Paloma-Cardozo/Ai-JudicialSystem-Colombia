The Case (2022/2023)

It started with a mom and her son.

"Blanca" filed a tutela on behalf of her son "Emilio," a child diagnosed with Autism Spectrum Disorder. The family was classified under SISBEN A4 (extreme poverty). She was asking for three things: no copayments for his therapies, transportation covered to get him to appointments, and a guarantee that his treatment wouldn't be cut off.

"Blanca" and "Emilio" are fictional names. Per Agreement 02 of 2015 and Internal Circular 10/2022, the Court publishes two versions of every ruling involving minors: one with real names sent only to the parties, and one with fictional names for public distribution. This is the public version.
DEC 2022

First instance court granted everything. EPS ordered to eliminate copayments and cover transport.

JAN 2023

Second instance confirmed, but the circuit court judge also opened ChatGPT 3.5 to "extend the arguments."

THEN

The AI's answers went straight into the official court record, word for word.

KEY FACT

The judge had already decided before consulting ChatGPT. The AI was used to write, not to think.

Source: Ruling T-323/2024, Constitutional Court of Colombia

The Court's Take

Ruling T-323/2024, Constitutional Court. Justice Juan Carlos Cortés González

✅

Due Process: NOT violated

The judge had already made up his mind before opening ChatGPT. The AI wrote; it didn't think. That order of events was exactly what saved him.

⚠️

Transparency: NOT met

Just saying "I used AI" isn't enough. The people whose case it was had a right to know which tool, what he asked it, and what the model can get wrong.

⚠️

Accountability: AT RISK

Some of what ChatGPT produced was wrong. It has no specialized training in Colombian law. Those errors ended up in an official court record, and nobody caught them.

✅ One thing he did get right: Emilio's personal data never went into ChatGPT. Privacy held.

What the Court Actually Ordered

Five binding orders in Ruling T-323/2024

1. Emilio's transportation coverage

The EPS must cover trips to every specialist visit and diagnostic procedure, not just therapy. For as long as the family can't pay for it themselves.

2. No more copayments, and no more runaround

The EPS has to coordinate with its own providers so exemptions under Laws 1438/2011 and 1618/2013 kick in automatically. A family in extreme poverty shouldn't have to fight for a right that already exists.

3. A message to every judge in Colombia

Not a ban. An exhortation: if you use AI, ask yourself whether it makes sense for the case and whether it holds up against constitutional principles and judicial independence.

4. Guidelines within four months

The Superior Council of Judicature had to issue AI rules built on the 12 principles in this ruling. They did it: Agreement PCSJA24-12243, December 2024.

5. Train every judge, starting now

The Judicial School must send this ruling to every judge in the country and put together ongoing AI training grounded in rights, not just technology. Next slide: the 12 principles the training has to cover.

Source: Ruling T-323/2024, Constitutional Court of Colombia

The 12 Principles

The Court didn't just point out what went wrong. It set out 12 principles that any use of AI in judicial work has to respect, and ordered the judiciary to build everything else on top of them.

These aren't suggestions. The Agreement PCSJA24-12243 is directly grounded in all twelve.

Source: Ruling T-323/2024, Constitutional Court of Colombia

What the Court Said Had to Change

Six principles ordered by the Court, later codified in Agreement PCSJA24-12243

👁️

Transparency

Put it on the record: which tool, what you asked, what it can and can't do

🎯

Accountability

Check everything the AI tells you. Get trained. The reasoning stays yours.

🔒

Privacy

No personal or sensitive data goes into any external AI tool. Full stop.

🧠

Non-Substitution

Weighing evidence and deciding a case is a human job. That doesn't change.

🔍

Rigor

Treat AI outputs the way you'd treat any unverified source: with skepticism

⚖️

Equality

Algorithmic bias is a real risk when rights are on the line

How We Got Here

From one court record with ChatGPT in it to a full regulatory framework, in under two years

AUG 2024

Ruling T-323/2024, Constitutional Court

The first AI ruling in Colombian justice history. It also told the Superior Council of Judicature to come up with concrete guidelines, and gave them four months to do it.

DEC 2024

Agreement PCSJA24-12243, Superior Council of Judicature

They delivered. 16 articles covering what's allowed, what isn't, who's responsible, and how to report usage. Three categories of permitted use, clearly defined.

FEB 2025

Survey: "AI Experiences in the Judiciary"

3,152 judicial servants answered questions about how they're actually using AI. The results (including an interactive dashboard) were made public.

MAY 2025

Judiciary + Ombudsman's Office

Both institutions started working groups together to review how the Agreement is being implemented, with a focus on human rights.

Sources: ramajudicial.gov.co, defensoria.gov.co, Ruling T-323/2024

What You Can and Cannot Do

Agreement PCSJA24-12243, Articles 4 and 5

✅ GO AHEAD

  • Write emails and administrative documents
  • Organize hearing schedules
  • Summarize case law, as long as a human checks it
  • Search for legal sources
  • Transcribe hearings
  • Fix grammar and spelling

❌ HARD NO

  • Let AI weigh evidence on its own
  • Use AI output as the reasoning behind a ruling
  • Let AI make any substantive call
  • Outsource the judge's thinking to a machine
  • Run case data through a free public chatbot
  • Feed personal data of any party into an AI

The Official 7-Step Checklist

CSJ's guide to using AI without getting into trouble (2024/2025)

1

Why are you using it?

Have a clear purpose before you open any tool

2

Is it actually allowed?

Check Article 4 of the Agreement before you start

3

Know your tool

Understand its limits and how it handles the data you give it

4

Remember the basics

No free chatbots with case data. Cite the AI. Protect personal data. The decision is yours.

5

You still have to check

Verify every piece of AI-generated information before it goes anywhere

6

Put it on the record

Log the tool, model, provider, date and the prompts you used. In the file.

7

Report it

Still a Work in Progress 🔭

🚧

No other country in LatAm has done this

Colombia is the first in the region to have a regulatory framework specifically for AI in the judiciary. The framework itself treats the rules as a starting point, not a final answer.

🤝

Nobody's doing this alone

The Judiciary, the Ombudsman's Office, and the Judicial School are working together on implementation. The Agreement also calls for collaboration with UNESCO and other international bodies.

🎯

Where they want to end up

A dedicated AI platform built for Colombian law. Not a general chatbot repurposed for court use. Fewer hallucinations, fewer biases, and no privacy gaps.
